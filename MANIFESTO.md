# What's Good Here — The Manifesto

*What this is. What it isn't. What it will never become.*

---

## The Problem

Every food platform has the same corruption built in. Restaurants pay for placement. Influencers get comped. Stars are gamed. The algorithm optimizes for engagement, not truth. By the time a tourist opens Yelp or Google, they're looking at a ranking that was bought, not earned.

The person who actually knows — the local who eats there every summer, the regular who's tried every dish on the menu, the carpenter who knows which lobster roll is worth $28 and which one isn't — their opinion is buried under paid ads and review farms.

That's the gap. Nobody has fixed it.

---

## What We're Building

A trust machine.

The score is the product. One question: **Would you order this again?** No stars, no vibes, no editorial. Just real people making a real call on a specific dish.

The value layer makes it sharper: **was it worth what you paid?** A 9.2 at $14 beats an 8.1 at $32. Always. Automatically. In a recession, in a tourist economy, in any city where pricing is predatory — the algorithm corrects for it. Good food at honest prices rises. Overpriced mediocrity sinks.

No restaurant can buy their way up. No influencer can skew it. No manager can delete bad reviews. The signal is what it is because real people ate the food and told the truth.

---

## Local Picks. Actually Local.

The product lives or dies on authenticity. Tourists don't need another algorithm that shows them the restaurant with the biggest marketing budget. They need the dish that locals keep ordering. The thing that makes someone say "you have to get the fried clams, not the chowder — trust me."

That's the transfer. Local knowledge, democratized. Tourists inherit the signal that locals built. Not because we told them to trust it — because the data is unmanipulable.

The moment we compromise that signal — sponsored results, boosted listings, "featured" placements — the product dies. Not eventually. Immediately. People will smell it.

**This will never happen.**

---

## The Flywheel

Restaurants that serve good food at fair prices rank higher.

Higher rankings mean more traffic.

More traffic means more votes.

More votes make the signal stronger.

Stronger signal brings more users.

More users mean more restaurants want in — not by paying, but by *earning* it.

This is the loop. It rewards the right behavior. It punishes extraction. It's how a market is supposed to work, before the intermediaries captured it.

---

## Who This Is For

**The tourist** who has one dinner on the island and doesn't want to waste it. They need one answer, fast, from someone who actually knows.

**The local** who's tired of watching mediocre tourist traps outrank the places that deserve it. They vote because it matters. Their opinion shapes what the next visitor finds.

**The restaurant** that does it right — good food, honest prices, no shortcuts — and has been invisible because they didn't pay for ads. They win here. Finally.

---

## The Line We Don't Cross

The score is sacred. Every feature, every partnership, every revenue model gets evaluated against one question:

**Does this compromise the signal?**

If yes: no. Full stop. We'd rather stay small than sell out. The value of this product is entirely contained in the trustworthiness of its rankings. That's it. That's everything.
